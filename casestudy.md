Riya, a second-year college student, actively searching for internships during her semester break, received an email that appeared to be from a well-known company offering a “paid internship opportunity.” The email looked professional, included a company logo, and mentioned that she had been “shortlisted.” It also created urgency by stating that she needed to confirm her application within 12 hours. 

Excited and anxious not to miss the opportunity, Riya clicked on the link provided in the email. She was redirected to a website that looked almost identical to the company’s official page. The form asked her to enter basic details, followed by her email login credentials “for verification purposes.” Without thinking much, she entered the information and submitted it.

Within a few hours, Riya noticed unusual activity. She received alerts about password reset attempts and unauthorized logins to her email and social media accounts. Soon, she was locked out of her own account.

Riya later realized that the email was a phishing attempt. The sender’s address had a slight spelling error, and legitimate companies never ask for login credentials. As a result, she faced stress, loss of access to important accounts, and risk of misuse of her personal data.

This scenario shows how urgency and trust can lead to quick decisions, making phishing attacks highly effective.
